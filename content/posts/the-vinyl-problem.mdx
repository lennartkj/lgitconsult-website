## The Numbers

Vinyl outsold CDs for the first time in decades in 2023 and the gap keeps growing. Film camera prices on the secondhand market have doubled. Fujifilm can't make Instax cartridges fast enough. Polaroid came back from the dead.

By any rational metric, these formats are worse. Vinyl is fragile, expensive, inconvenient, lower fidelity than lossless digital. Film costs per frame, requires processing, can't match modern sensor resolution. They persist anyway.

## Convenience Ate Meaning

Digital media solved distribution so completely that it created a new problem: when everything is available instantly and infinitely, nothing feels valuable. A hundred million songs on Spotify is objectively better than 200 records on a shelf. But the shelf means something. Each record is a choice, a purchase, a physical commitment.

The vinyl problem in one sentence: convenience removes friction, and friction was carrying meaning.

The parallel to digital product design is direct. Strip every friction point — instant loading, infinite scroll, zero-commitment interactions — and you often strip the sense of substance too. The smoothest experience isn't always the most valuable one.

## Objects Have Presence

A record has weight. A large-format cover you can see without zooming. Liner notes you can read. You pull it from the sleeve, place it on the turntable, drop the needle. The ritual is the experience.

Digital files have no presence. They're database entries with thumbnails. Playing a rare B-side feels identical to playing the number-one hit. The interface flattens everything.

When we design digital experiences, we're fighting against this inherent weightlessness. Physical design has gravity, texture, wear — all signals of value and history. Digital design has to manufacture those signals through typography, spacing, motion, and density.

## Imperfection as Proof

Surface noise. Film grain. Tape hiss. Letterpress impression. Technically flaws — and actively sought out today.

The appeal isn't just nostalgia. Imperfection signals process. Surface noise means a needle is tracing a groove. Grain means light hit a chemical emulsion. The artifact proves the method. Digital production erases those traces entirely.

For creative work, this is practical: showing process builds connection. A design that reveals how it was made — visible grid lines, intentional roughness, systematic numbering — creates more engagement than one that hides its construction behind polish.

## The Takeaway for Digital

We use Next.js, not letterpress. But the vinyl problem is a useful lens: does this feel like something or nothing? Does the interface have weight, or is it another frictionless scroll?

Sometimes the answer is a pacing choice — making someone wait before a reveal. Sometimes it's typography with enough scale to feel monumental. Sometimes it's a glitch effect that reminds you there's a system underneath.

Ease and value are different things. The formats that persist — vinyl, film, print — persist because they deliver value through friction.
