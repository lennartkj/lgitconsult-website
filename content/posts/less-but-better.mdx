## Weniger, aber besser

Less, but better. Dieter Rams never meant it as a slogan. He meant it as a constraint — and over two decades at Braun he proved it was the harder discipline, not the lazy one. Anyone can add. Restraint demands that you know exactly what to remove, and then have the nerve to stop.

That nerve is the whole thing. A Braun object from the 1960s looks inevitable, as if it could not have been otherwise — which is the surest sign of how many decisions were made and then hidden. The work that went in is the work you cannot see.

## Snow White's Coffin

In 1956 Rams and Hans Gugelot did something quietly radical: they put a clear acrylic lid on a record player. The SK4 — nicknamed *Schneewittchensarg*, Snow White's Coffin — replaced the heavy wooden cabinet everyone expected with a white metal case and a transparent top. It refused to pretend to be furniture. It was a machine, and it said so, beautifully.

What followed was a decade most design careers never touch. The T3 pocket radio, whose face Jony Ive would lift almost wholesale onto the iPod forty years later. The ET66 calculator, still legible in the one on your phone. The 606 Universal Shelving System, drawn in 1960 and still in production through Vitsœ — a thing you buy once and add to for the rest of your life. Rams later distilled it into ten principles, the last and most quoted of which is the one everyone forgets to practice: *good design is as little design as possible.*

## Restraint as the Signal

This is why the eye keeps returning to Braun, and why it belongs in this conversation at all. We are entering an age of infinite generation — design, image, object, all producible at zero cost and in endless variation. When addition is free, addition stops meaning anything. The loud object no longer signals effort, or money, or care, because loudness has become the cheapest thing to manufacture.

Restraint is what cannot be faked at scale. A disciplined object carries the evidence of a decision *not* to add — and that decision is expensive in the only currency that still counts: judgment. It is why a mass-produced 1965 Braun shaver can sit in a room and quietly outclass almost everything designed since. It was never about rarity. It was about someone knowing when to stop, and the eye can feel that across sixty years.

## What the Eye Takes

You do not collect Braun to own something scarce — you can still find most of it cheap, often mislabelled, in the corners of German estate sales and classifieds. You collect it to keep a standard in the room: a fixed point of *less, but better* to measure everything else against.

That is the whole education. Train your eye on the object that knew when to stop, and the noise of everything that didn't becomes obvious.
