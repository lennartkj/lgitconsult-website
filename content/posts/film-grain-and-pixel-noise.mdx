## The Texture Gap

Open most modern websites and you'll notice something missing: texture. Flat color fields, sharp vector edges, perfectly anti-aliased type. Everything is clean in a way that real materials never are. Paper has grain. Film has noise. Concrete has surface. Screens, by default, have none of this.

That absence is a design choice, even when it's not a conscious one. And it's worth questioning.

## Why Grain Matters

Film grain isn't a flaw — it's information. Each frame of 35mm or medium-format film carries a unique pattern of silver halide crystals that responded to light in a chemically unpredictable way. No two frames have the same grain structure. The image is literally embedded in a material.

Digital sensors produce noise too, but we've spent decades engineering it away. High ISO noise reduction, pixel binning, computational photography — all designed to produce cleaner, smoother output. The result is technically superior and aesthetically sterile.

When designers add grain overlays to digital images, they're reintroducing that material quality. It's not about making something look "vintage." It's about giving the eye something to hold onto — a surface beneath the image that reminds you this was made, not generated.

## Grain in Interface Design

This principle extends beyond photography. Interface design has the same flatness problem. A subtle noise texture over a background, barely visible at normal viewing distances, changes how a surface feels without changing how it reads. It's the difference between a painted wall and a printed sheet of paper — same color, different experience.

We use this on our own site: a grain overlay on image treatments that breaks the digital perfection. It's set to a low opacity and composited as a multiply layer. Most visitors won't consciously notice it. They'll just feel that the images have more presence than a typical web-optimized JPEG.

## The CRT Echo

There's a parallel in how we relate to screens. CRT monitors had visible scan lines, phosphor glow, and slight color bleeding at edges. LCDs eliminated all of that in favor of uniform, pixel-perfect output. Now creative tools like Cavalry and After Effects ship with CRT simulation effects, and indie games use scan line shaders as an aesthetic choice.

This isn't purely nostalgia. CRT artifacts created a visual warmth that modern displays lack. The imperfection was part of the character. When we add scan lines to a digital composition, we're acknowledging that perfection isn't always the goal — sometimes character is.

## Finding the Balance

The risk with any texture overlay is pastiche. Slapping an Instagram-era film filter on a photo doesn't make it analog — it makes it derivative. The goal is subtlety: texture that serves the composition rather than dominating it.

The question worth asking on every project: does this surface need texture to feel real? Sometimes the answer is no — data dashboards and technical interfaces benefit from clinical clarity. But for work that needs to feel human, crafted, or physical, a little grain goes a long way.

Material doesn't have to mean physical. It just has to mean intentional.
