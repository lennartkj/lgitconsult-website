## Flat by Default

Most websites have no texture. Flat color, vector edges, anti-aliased type. Paper has grain, film has noise, concrete has surface. Screens have none of this unless you put it there.

That absence is a choice, even when it's accidental.

## What Grain Actually Is

Film grain is silver halide crystals responding to light. Each frame has a unique pattern — the image is embedded in a physical material. Digital noise is the sensor equivalent, but we've spent decades engineering it out. High ISO noise reduction, computational photography, pixel binning — all aimed at cleaner output.

When designers overlay grain on digital images, they're not doing retro pastiche. They're reintroducing material evidence. The texture says: this came from a process. It wasn't generated — it was made.

## Surface in Interfaces

The same principle works in UI design. A subtle noise texture over a background, barely visible at normal distance, changes how a surface feels without changing how it reads. Painted wall versus printed paper — same color, different experience.

We use a grain overlay on image treatments across this site. Low opacity, multiply blend mode. Most visitors won't notice it consciously. The images just have more weight than a standard web JPEG.

## The CRT Parallel

CRTs had scan lines, phosphor glow, slight color bleed. LCDs eliminated all of it. Now CRT simulation effects ship in motion design tools and indie games use scan line shaders on purpose. Not nostalgia — character. The imperfection was part of what made the medium feel specific.

## Where It Breaks

The risk is pastiche. An Instagram-era film filter doesn't make a photo analog — it makes it derivative. Grain has to serve the composition, not sit on top of it.

The test: does this surface need texture to feel real? Data dashboards don't. Campaign imagery does. Interface chrome usually doesn't. Hero images often do. Match the treatment to the content.
