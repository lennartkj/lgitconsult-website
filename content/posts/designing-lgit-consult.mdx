## The Problem

Our previous site looked like every other tech consultancy. Blue accent, rounded corners, centered headings over card grids. Functional. Forgettable. It said "SaaS startup" when it should have said "creative agency in Leipzig that actually makes things."

The reference points were publications like 032c, Ssense Editorial, the Virgil Abloh playbook — not Stripe's landing page. We needed editorial tension, not conversion optimization.

## Strip the Color

First decision: kill the blue. The entire palette went monochrome — warm off-white (`#f8f7f4`) and near-black (`#111111`). Color comes from photography and project work. The interface stays neutral.

This forced everything else. Without color to create hierarchy, typography had to carry the entire visual system. That constraint turned out to be the most productive one.

## Type Carries Everything

Geist Sans at light weight, large sizes, tight tracking for display. Geist Mono at 11px with wide letter-spacing for metadata and labels. The contrast between a thin 72px headline and a tiny mono label does more work than any color palette could.

The index numbering — `001 — Services`, `002 — Work` — came from the glitch effect's existing numbering system. We just surfaced it into the typography and made the connection explicit.

## Kill the Card

The old card had `rounded-lg border shadow-sm`. Standard. We replaced it with ruled lines and sharp edges. Content sits on the page with 1px dividers, like a printed spread. Hover states shift elements horizontally rather than lifting them with shadows.

## Break the Grid

Every section used to be centered heading, paragraph, three-column grid. Now it's 12-column layouts with intentional asymmetry — content in columns 1–7, sidebar in 9–12. Heroes are left-aligned with vertical space doing the heavy lifting. Different sections use different column distributions.

## The Glitch as System

The canvas-based glitch effect was already drawing circles, lines, and index numbers on scroll. Rather than treating it as decoration, we made it structural — the same numbering system appears in section labels, the same ruled-line logic appears in card layouts. The glitch isn't an overlay. It's the design language.

## What Stayed

Next.js 15, Tailwind, Framer Motion, MDX for content. The architecture was already solid. The redesign was purely visual — new paint on a good frame. Seven phases over six weeks, each one removing something that felt safe: a shadow, a rounded corner, a centered layout. What's left is more intentional.
