## The Look We're After

We shoot on film for most of our campaign and editorial work. Not as a statement. Not because analog is inherently better. Because we're after a specific image quality — the tonal rolloff, the grain structure, the way highlights compress on Portra 400 — and the fastest way to get that look faithfully is to just shoot the format that produces it natively.

Could we approximate it digitally? Sure. Capture on a mirrorless body, run it through VSCO or Dehancer, add grain in post. We've tested it extensively. The results get close but never quite land. The highlight behavior is wrong. The grain sits on top of the image instead of living inside it. The color transitions are too clean. You spend three hours in Lightroom chasing something a roll of Kodak delivers out of the scanner.

So we skip the approximation and do the real thing. It's not romantic — it's efficient.

## Reprint, Rescan, Repeat

One of our standard techniques: shoot film, develop it, make an initial scan. Then print the scan on specific paper stock — usually a matte with visible tooth — and rescan the print. Sometimes we do this twice. Each generation adds something the digital pipeline can't replicate: the paper's texture embeds into the image, micro-variations in the print head create subtle inconsistencies, the scanner picks up a different tonal curve than the original negative.

The result is an image that looks like it was pulled from a 1970s magazine archive. Rich, slightly degraded, with a material quality that's impossible to fake convincingly in Photoshop. We use this for album artwork, brand campaigns, social media content — anywhere the visual needs to feel like it has history.

This isn't a gimmick. It's a reproducible production technique. Same paper, same printer settings, same scanner profile. Consistent results, specific aesthetic. The analog steps are just tools in the chain.

## Whiteboards Are Faster Than Figma

For layout and architecture work, we start on the whiteboard in our Leipzig studio. A marker and a wall-sized surface lets you make structural decisions in seconds — hierarchy, flow, section order, content priority. No layers panel. No alignment guides. No temptation to start picking typefaces when you should be deciding what goes where.

The fidelity of the medium matches the fidelity of the decisions. At the whiteboard stage, we're answering questions like "does this section exist" and "what comes first." Those are structural calls. Getting them wrong costs days in code. Getting them right takes ten minutes with a marker.

We move to Figma once the structure is locked. Not before.

## Physical Reference Walls

We keep a reference wall in the studio. Printed photographs, magazine tearsheets, material samples, type specimens. For each new project, relevant references go up on the wall where they're visible all day — not buried in a Figma mood board that gets opened once during a kickoff call and never again.

A printed photograph at actual size has presence that a thumbnail on a screen doesn't. You notice texture, color relationships, how type works at real dimensions. When the references are physical and persistent — on the wall every time you look up from your screen — they inform decisions passively. The project absorbs the references without anyone having to schedule a "mood board review."

## Mockups Before Pixels

For campaign work, we build physical mockups before opening any design software. Print the elements, cut them out, arrange them on a table, photograph the arrangement with a phone. Twenty minutes. The result is a rough comp that tells us whether the concept works — whether the composition communicates, whether the hierarchy is clear, whether the pieces relate to each other the right way.

This separates the creative decisions from the technical ones. Moving printed pieces on a table means thinking purely about composition and message. Inside InDesign, you're simultaneously thinking about composition, bleed settings, CMYK values, paragraph styles, and export presets. The physical step strips the technical noise so the creative call is cleaner.

## The Point

Every analog step in our process exists because it produces a better result faster, not because the format is sacred. Film gives us a look we can't faithfully replicate digitally. The reprint-rescan chain gives us a material quality that Photoshop filters can't match. Whiteboards give us structural clarity before we've committed to pixels. Physical mockups give us creative confidence before we've committed to production.

The output is always digital. The website ships as Next.js. The campaign delivers as JPEGs and MP4s. The brand guidelines live in a PDF. But the path through analog tools at specific stages produces work that feels different — more considered, more textured, more specific — than an all-digital pipeline.

We're not choosing between analog and digital. We're choosing the right tool at each stage for what that stage requires.
